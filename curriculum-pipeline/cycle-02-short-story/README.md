# Curriculum Pipeline — Cycle 02: Short Story
## ELA 8 | Spring 2026

> **Status: In Progress.** Student work samples and analysis will be added as the unit develops.

---

## Overview

This unit tasks students with writing an original short story from scratch. It is structured as a
multi-week drafting cycle, moving students through story structure, rising action, scene-level
craft, and voice — one manageable stage at a time.

Two dimensions of this unit are central to the broader portfolio:

1. **Professional collaboration.** The unit's structural framework was contributed by a
   published author and Big Island resident who visits the classroom twice per week to give
   students direct feedback and brainstorming support.

2. **Philosophy-aligned use of educational technology.** This is the first complete unit whose
   materials were built in full accordance with the teaching philosophy documented in
   `portfolios/2026/philosophy/manifesto.md`. All digital templates were generated using
   Claude Code with the teacher as the sole operator and are designed to be invisible
   infrastructure: students interact only with the printed worksheet, never with the tool that
   produced it.

## Professional Collaboration

The unit's narrative framework was written by a published author who lives and works on the
Big Island. She visits twice per week to work directly with students: reading drafts, asking
questions, helping students push past stuck points in their stories.

Her contribution to the unit materials is the story outline document
(`expert-resources/260323_story outline.pdf`), a 22-step narrative structure she developed
through her own work as a writer. It is the backbone of the entire unit's sequencing. The
outline moves a story from opening setting through to epilogue, using names for each stage
that balance craft vocabulary with accessibility:

```
Set the setting            →   Introduce the MC(s)        →   Inciting Incident
Defining Moment            →   Call to Action             →   1st Plot Point
First Pinch Point          →   Falling down the rocky hill →  New Knowledge
Grace Period               →   That was nice, but...      →   Retreat
2nd Pinch Point            →   Death Experience / Crisis  →   2nd Plot Point
Wake Up and Smell the Coffee → Transformation             →   Decision
Grand Gesture              →   Climax                     →   Resolution
Epilogue
```

Each step is defined by a question the writer must answer — for example:

```
Inciting Incident:   What external event causes the MC(s) to act or react in a way
                     that launches the story?

1st Plot Point:      What No-Turning-Back moment changes everything, launching the MC(s)
                     into a new world / environment / viewpoint that makes them question
                     everything they thought they knew?

Death Experience:    What happens to cause the MC(s) to fear they won't survive —
                     physically, emotionally, or spiritually?

Wake Up and Smell    What event gives the MC(s) the insight they need to prevail?
the Coffee:
```

In addition to the document, the guest author has produced several large, hand-drawn and colored
posters that live in the classroom and give students visual reference points for story structure,
character motivation, and the shape of a rising action. These function as persistent,
low-tech scaffolds that reinforce the same ideas students encounter in their worksheets.

The templates in this unit were produced by giving Claude Code existing teaching documents,
rubrics, and instructional context — then reviewing and refining the output until each sheet
matched the pedagogical intent. Students receive printed worksheets. They do not know, and do
not need to know, that a language model produced the layout.

This is a direct application of principles 11 and 15: the teacher operated the tool; the
students received its output as curriculum. The AI served as a production accelerant, not a
replacement for instructional judgment. Every decision about what skills to target, what
questions to ask, and what the connective tissue reference guide should say came from the
teacher and from collaboration with the author. Claude Code rendered those decisions into
printable form.

## Unit Materials

### `expert-resources/`

| File | Description |
|---|---|
| `260323_story outline.pdf` | The author's 22-step story structure framework. Defines each stage with a guiding question. Used as the structural backbone for sequencing student drafting across the unit. |

### `templates/`

These are reusable HTML templates, printable and designed to be filled out by hand. Each
template is generated from the teacher's instructions and populated with cohort-specific
content before printing.

---

#### `three_pass_draft_sheet_template.html`

The core weekly worksheet. Each printed instance covers one stage of the story outline. The
sheet is divided into three sections, each targeting a discrete writing skill:

| Section | Name | Skill |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | This Week's Task | Teacher-written context explaining what story stage students are in and what their job is this week. Filled in fresh each week before printing. |
| 1 | Sequence It | Logical sequencing. Students answer 3–4 planning questions about what happens in this stage of their story, ending with a cause-and-effect summary sentence. |
| 2 | Show It | Showing. Students write one moment from their sequence as a scene — no summarizing, no stating emotions. A self-checklist prompts them to verify action, sensory detail, body language, and in-scene prose before moving on. |
| 3 | Voice It | Voice. Students rewrite a passage from Section 2 with attention to their character's voice and at least one line of dialogue. A sentence-length challenge asks them to place a short sentence beside a long one and read them aloud. |

The three-pass structure (plan → show → voice) is designed to prevent students from conflating
skill targets. Each pass asks for only one thing.

---

#### `rising_action_connective_tissue.html`

A single-page reference guide titled *"What Holds the Rising Action Together?"* Used when
students reach the rising action stages of the outline and need a way to think about why one
event follows from another.

The sheet presents seven possible connective threads, each in a color-coded card:

| Thread | Description |
|---|---|
| The Antagonist | A person, force, or system that keeps getting in the way — not once, but again and again. |
| The MC's Flaw | The MC keeps failing for the same reason. Their weakness shapes every decision. |
| The Central Mystery | A question the reader can't stop thinking about. Each event reveals a clue or deepens it. |
| A Key Relationship | A friendship, rivalry, or alliance that grows or breaks under pressure. |
| A Ticking Clock | A deadline that makes every failure feel more costly. |
| Cause and Effect | One event causes the next. The MC's response to each problem creates a new, bigger one. |
| A Recurring Place or System | The same location keeps appearing, but meaning something different each time. |

Each card includes a definition and a *Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone* example. The
bottom note reads: *"Your story doesn't need all seven. Pick one or two that fit your MC's
situation and let them run through your whole rising action."*
