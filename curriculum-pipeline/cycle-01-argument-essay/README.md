# Curriculum Pipeline — Cycle 01: Argument Essay
## *Frankenstein* (Gris Grimly adaptation) | ELA 8 | Fall 2025

---

## Overview

This document traces the instructional arc of a five-assignment sequence designed to scaffold
students from single-paragraph evidence writing to a full five-paragraph argument essay. The
anchor text was the Gris Grimly graphic novel adaptation of Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein*. The
driving question across the unit was: **Is the Creature human or not?**

Two student profiles are included to illustrate differentiated pathways through the pipeline:

- **Standard** — Standard student
- **ELL** — English Language Learner

Each assignment built directly on the last. The final essays were selected because they
demonstrated clear uptake of the skills introduced in each preceding task.

---

## Assignment Sequence

```
1. Evidence-Based Paragraph
2. Argument Essay Escape Room
3. Gathering Quotes               [ELL only]
4. Argument Lesson: Rebuttal      [phone scan / notebook]
5. Final Argument Essay
```

---

## 1. Evidence-Based Paragraph

**Assignment intent:** Before introducing the full essay, students practiced the foundational
move of an argument body paragraph: state a claim, cite a quote from the text, and explain
what that evidence shows. This task isolated the use-of-evidence skill from the structural
demands of a multi-paragraph essay.

The standard prompt asked students to respond to: *Why does Victor destroy the mate he was
creating right after seeing the creature's face in the window? Include evidence from the text,
and be sure to explain what each piece of evidence shows.*

The ELL version was more heavily scaffolded: students received sentence starters and a short
list of pre-selected quotes to choose from.

---

```
I think he does this because he is reminded of all the havoc and misfortune the male creature
has brought up on him and his family already, when the creature appears in the window. He
realized what a dumb idea it was to try to create another one. He had finally gotten out of the
hard time he was having before, when he was grieving and feeling guilty over the loss of his

"Three years before I was engaged in the same manner, and had created a fiend whose
```

## 2. Argument Essay Escape Room

**Assignment intent:** Before drafting, students needed a working mental model of how an
argument essay is structured. This activity gamified structural knowledge: students sorted
twelve components of an argument essay into the correct sequence, then used those answers
to decode a three-number lock combination. Getting the structure right was the literal key to
advancing. 

This assignment was gamified: the order of the correct answers could be used by partner pairs
to unlock a 3-digit combination suitcase that contained a prize. Students completed the task ferociously. 

The twelve components covered:
- Hook
- Thesis / main idea statement
- Counterargument frame ("While some say ___, actually ___.")
- Two body paragraphs, each with: topic sentence → evidence → analysis
- Conclusion: restatement → point summaries → closing thought

---

### Standard (Standard)

Standard produced the correct sequence:

```
1.  Hook to grab the readers' attention
2.  State main idea and summarize points.
3.  "While some say ___, actually ______."
4.  Restate the main question and introduce point 1.
5.  Cite evidence for point 1.
6.  Explain why the evidence is relevant to point 1.
7.  Explain why the evidence is relevant to point 1.   [second analysis sentence]
8.  Cite evidence for point 2.
9.  Explain why the evidence is relevant for point 2.
10. Start the conclusion paragraph by restating the question and summarizing the points.
11. Include brief explanations of points 1 and 2 in the conclusion paragraph.
12. Write a final sentence to end the essay on a thoughtful note.
```



---

### ELL (ELL)

ELL's ordering contained several errors. She placed the thesis before the hook, inserted an
evidence explanation at step 3 before the counterargument had appeared, and did not produce
an explanation for point 2's evidence:

```
1.  State the main idea and summarize points.          [should follow the hook]
2.  Hook to grab the readers' attention                [should be #1]
3.  Explain why the evidence is relevant to point 1.   [premature — no evidence cited yet]
4.  "While some say ___, actually ______."
5.  Cite evidence for point 1.
6.  Restate the main question and introduce point 1.
7.  Explain why the evidence is relevant to point 1.
8.  Restate the main question and introduce point 2.
9.  Cite evidence for point 2.
10. Start the conclusion paragraph by restating the question and summarizing the points.
11. Include brief explanations of points 1 and 2 in the conclusion paragraph.
12. Write a final sentence to end the essay on a thoughtful note.
```

The errors at steps 1–3 suggest uncertainty about the introduction's internal order, and the
absence of a step for explaining point 2's evidence foreshadows the missing rebuttal/analysis
on her next assignment.

## 3. Gathering Quotes *(ELL only)*

**Assignment intent:** This pre-writing task gave ELL a structured opportunity to return to
*Frankenstein* with her essay prompt in mind, select direct quotes, record page numbers, and
articulate in writing how each quote supported her thinking. The goal was to decouple the work
of finding and reading evidence from the pressure of drafting prose — a step standard students
were expected to complete more independently.

ELL selected the prompt: **"The creature is human or not."**

| Direct Quote | How This Quote Supports My Opinion |
|---|---|
| "I look around and I have no relation or friend upon earth" | "This shows that he is not a human because human does have family and friends and he don't have anyone" |
| "My heart was fashioned to be susceptible of love and sympathy" | "This shows that he is a human because he can feel love. A non-human monster would not have the same feelings." |
| "Of my creation and creator I was absolutely ignorant, but I knew that I possessed no money, no friends, no kind of property, I was not even of the same nature as man." | "This shows that he is not a human but at the same this he is a human so it makes me be between about what he actually is" |

*Score: 19/25 (Rubric: Intro 4/5 | Body ¶1 5/5 | Body ¶2 5/5 | Rebuttal 0/5 | Conclusion 5/5)*

All three quotes ELL collected here appear **verbatim** in her final essay. The analysis she
wrote in the right-hand column maps directly onto her body paragraph explanations. Notably,
her third-row analysis — in which she says the quote makes her "be between about what he
actually is" — becomes the thesis of her entire essay.

## 4. Argument Lesson: Rebuttal *(Phone Scan / Notebook)*

**Assignment intent:** The rebuttal was the paragraph ELL scored 0/5 on in the gathering
quotes rubric, and the step her escape room ordering suggested she hadn't fully internalized.
This lesson addressed that gap directly. Students recorded a four-step rebuttal formula in their
notebooks, identified one strong opposition claim for their topic, then practiced writing a full
rebuttal paragraph applying the formula:

```
1. Restate the opposition's claim.
2. Assume it's true.
3. "If this is true, then ___." (follow the logic to a consequence)
4. Conclude you're correct. (show the consequence is false or absurd)
```

---

The notebook entry below (dated 12/9/25) shows the formula alongside a student's practice
paragraph. The opposition claim identified:

```
The creature is not human because it was assembled from many parts.
```

The full rebuttal paragraph written in the notebook:

```
My opposition believes that Creature is human because he feels sympathy and compassion.
Let's assume it's true that having compassion makes you human. If this is true, then dolphins,
which show compassion by their actions, must be human. Dolphins are not human, and
therefore this idea is incorrect. Having compassion does not make the creature human.
```

## 5. Final Argument Essay

**Assignment intent:** Students wrote a full argument essay responding to: **Is the creature in
*Frankenstein* human or not?** The essays included here were chosen because each student
demonstrably drew on skills built in the preceding assignments: correct essay structure,
textual evidence with explanation, and a rebuttal paragraph.

---

### Standard (Standard) — "Is The Creature Human Or Not?"

**Thesis:** The creature is human.

**Structure (matching her escape room sequence):**
- Hook → background → thesis
- Body 1: Counterargument + rebuttal (human DNA evidence)
- Body 2: Emotional evidence
- Body 3: Behavioral evidence
- Conclusion: Summary + thematic closing line

---

**Skill callback — Evidence paragraph:** Standard's evidence paragraph had flagged "does this"
as too vague for an opening claim. Compare the openers:

*Evidence paragraph:*
```
I think he does this because he is reminded of all the havoc and misfortune the male
creature has brought up on him and his family already...
```

*Final essay, Body 2:*
```
Another reason I have to support my beliefs of him being a human is the way he has
feelings just like human beings have.
```

**Skill callback — Escape room structure:** The essay opens with a hook, moves into thesis,
then immediately addresses the counterargument before the evidence paragraphs — exactly
the sequence Standard correctly ordered in the escape room:

```
Have you ever felt judged by someone who didn't even know you? In class, we read a book
from 1818 called Frankenstein by Gris Grimly. The story is about a man named Victor
Frankenstein and his "creation." [...] Having said this, I still think he's a human.

Many people would say he's a monster because he doesn't fit the exact human criteria.
Although he's tall and seems like a monster, if this were true then he wouldn't have human
DNA. He does, therefore he is human. On page 37 it says, "I collected bones from charnel
houses; and disturbed, with profane fingers, the tremendous secrets of the human frame."
So although he seems like a monster, I believe he's a human.
```

**Evidence paragraphs:**

```
Another reason I have to support my beliefs of him being a human is the way he has
feelings just like human beings have. [...] On page 117 the creature says, "I swear to you
that, with the companion you bestow, I will quit the neighborhood of man." He says this
because he just wants to be accepted by someone and be happy.
```

```
My final reason for believing the creature to be human is his behavior. He walks, talks and
functions as if he were a human. [...] On page 95 the creature said, "What chiefly struck me
was the gentle manners of these people; and I longed to join them, but dared not."
```

**Conclusion:**
```
Between all of these reasons, they have led me to believe he is human. The creature has a
human anatomy, and he looks human too. He has human emotions, feelings, he acts like a
human and seems like a human overall. This story is a reminder to not judge a book by its
cover and read it all the way through instead.
```

**Teacher Feedback:**
This student chose to go for the extra credit assignment and include a formal rebuttal in her argument essay. 
She in particular wanted feedback and instruction verbally in the form of one on one discussions. 

In the end, this student chose to modify the structure of the essay in service of confluence with her overall 
narrative style: she started her first body paragraph as a rebuttal rather than waiting for paragraph 3. 
Having already produced clear and strong claims in her introduction, I agreed with this stylistic choice. 

This student received full credit for this assignment. 

---

### ELL (ELL) — "Is the creature Human?"

**Thesis:** Both sides — ELL argues that she is "conflicted between both ideas."

**Structure:**
- Background intro → dual-sided thesis
- Body 1: Not human (isolation / social connection evidence)
- Body 2: Human (emotional evidence)
- Body 3: Conflicted (self-awareness / nature evidence)
- Rebuttal paragraph
- Conclusion

---

**Skill callback — Gathering quotes:** All three quotes ELL collected in the pre-writing table
appear in her final essay, used in the same interpretive direction as her analysis column.

*Gathering quotes, row 1:*
```
Quote:    "I look around and I have no relation or friend upon earth"
Analysis: "This shows that he is not a human because human does have family and friends
           and he don't have anyone"
```

*Final essay, Body 1:*
```
The creature from Victor Frankenstein is not a human because he lacks the basic social
connections that all humans naturally have. In the novel he says, "I look around and I have
no friends or relation upon earth." This quote shows that he has nobody—no family, no
friends, and no community. Humans are born into families, and even if they lose them, they
usually form new relationships.
```

---

*Gathering quotes, row 2:*
```
Quote:    "My heart was fashioned to be susceptible of love and sympathy"
Analysis: "This shows that he is a human because he can feel love. A non-human monster
           would not have the same feelings."
```

*Final essay, Body 2:*
```
Even though the creature seems non-human in some ways, he also shows feelings and
emotions that prove he is human. One example is when he says, "My heart was fashioned
to be susceptible to love and sympathy." These emotions of love, sympathy, and compassion
are deeply human traits. A non-human monster would not be able to feel or understand love
the way he does.
```

---

*Gathering quotes, row 3:*
```
Quote:    "Of my creation and creator I was absolutely ignorant, but I knew that I possessed
           no money, no friends, no kind of property, I was not even of the same nature as man."
Analysis: "This shows that he is not a human but at the same this he is a human so it makes
           me be between about what he actually is"
```

*Final essay, Body 3:*
```
Because the creature shows both human and non-human qualities, I find myself in the
middle, unsure of what he truly is. He explains, "Of my creation and creator I possessed
no money, no friends, no kind of property, I was not even of the same nature as man."
This quote makes me feel conflicted. On one hand, he knows he is different and not the
same "nature" as humans. On the other hand, he speaks with self awareness and motion,
which are extremely human traits.
```

---

**Skill callback — Rebuttal lesson:** The rebuttal paragraph in the final essay applies the
four-step formula from the notebook lesson. The argument has been adapted from the practice
paragraph (dolphins/compassion) to fit ELL's specific claim (creature-as-both-things), but the
logical structure — *assume → if true, then → absurd consequence → therefore incorrect* — is
identical:

*Notebook practice (12/9):*
```
My opposition believes that Creature is human because he feels sympathy and compassion.
Let's assume it's true that having compassion makes you human. If this is true, then dolphins,
which show compassion by their actions, must be human. Dolphins are not human, and
therefore this idea is incorrect. Having compassion does not make the creature human.
```

*Final essay, Rebuttal:*
```
Some people say that the creature can't be both human and not human. Let's assume it is
true that a living thing can't be more than one species. In that case, mules wouldn't be able
to exist. However, mules do exist. Therefore, the idea that mules don't exist is wrong.
```

**Conclusion:**
```
In Frankenstein, the creature challenges our ideas of what it means to be human. Some
evidence shows he is not human because he had no natural relationships and was created
unnaturally. Other evidence shows he is human because he feels love, sympathy, and
emotional pain. After looking at both sides, I feel stuck in the middle. The creature is too
human to be a monster, but too different to be fully human. Shelley's novel leaves readers
questioning identity, belonging, and what truly makes someone human.
```

**Teacher Feedback:**
This student showed good understanding of the concepts in her final product, but needed support while writing. 
On multiple occasions throughout the writing process, the student raised their hand to ask basic questions, 
such as "what should my essay even be about?"

Knowing she is highly capable of following direct instruction, I explained that if she is stuck on the question
of the Creature's humanity, she could represent that in writing by following the following structure:
1) Intro Paragraph
2) Reason for
3) Reason against
4) Formal-style rebuttal
5) Conclusion. 

Feedback with this student proceeded by allowing her to write independently and answering her specific questions
as she went along. She would often simply ask, "is this paragraph good enough?" I would always reply by walking 
through my checklist of steps for the respective paragraph out loud. This would always clear things up.

Knowing that this option was worth extra credit, she followed the instructions to a T, and got nearly full credit for
the final product. The only final criticism was one point taken off for her use of the phrase "in this essay, ..."
which I had prohibited due to its stylistic uselessness. Otherwise, I think her work was fantastic and exemplary, 
even among her non-ELL peers.
